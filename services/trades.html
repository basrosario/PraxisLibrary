Estimates & Quotes

Win more jobs with professional quotes.

Professional Estimate BuilderCOSTAR
[Context] You're helping a [trade type] create professional estimates that win jobs and protect the business.
[Objective] Create a detailed estimate for [type of job] that clearly explains the work, justifies the price, and sets proper expectations.
[Style] Professional, clear, no jargon the customer won't understand.
[Tone] Confident, trustworthy, straightforward.
[Audience] Homeowner/property manager making a decision on who to hire.
[Response] Create an estimate including: (1) Header Information - business name/contact, customer information, date/estimate number, valid until date; (2) Scope of Work - clear description of work, specific locations/items addressed, exclusions (what's NOT included); (3) Line Items - labor (hours x rate or flat), materials (itemized or allowance), equipment/fees, permits if required; (4) Terms & Conditions - payment terms, timeline, warranty info, customer prep requirements; (5) Total - clear total price, deposit required, change order policy.
[Job Details] Type of work: [Describe the job]. Materials needed: [List materials]. Labor estimate: [Hours or day rate]. Your pricing: [What you want to charge]. Special conditions: [Permits, prep work, etc.].
[Constraints] Clear enough customer knows what they're getting. Detailed enough to prevent scope disputes. Professional presentation. Include warranty and terms. Make it easy to say yes.
Estimate Follow-Up MessageCRISP
[Context] I'm a [trade professional] following up on an estimate I sent [X] days ago. I want to close the job without being pushy.
[Request] Write a follow-up message that checks in on my estimate and encourages them to move forward.
[Instructions] Job type: [What was the estimate for]. Days since sent: [How long ago]. Last contact: [Any conversations since]. Competition: [Do they have other quotes]. Include: (1) Friendly check-in; (2) Address common concerns (timing, questions); (3) Create gentle urgency (schedule filling up, prices may change); (4) Clear call to action; (5) Option to chat if they have questions.
[Style] Professional but human—not salesy.
[Parameters] Keep it brief (they're busy). Make response easy (yes/no or call back). Include your phone number.
[Constraints] Don't be pushy. Show you care about helping, not just selling. Acknowledge they might be getting other quotes. Leave the door open if now isn't right.

Customer Communication

Professional communication that builds trust.

Job Update TemplatesCRISP
[Context] I run a [trade] business and need professional text/email templates for keeping customers informed throughout a job.
[Request] Create a set of customer communication templates I can quickly use on job sites.
[Instructions] Create templates for: (1) Appointment confirmation; (2) On my way/ETA; (3) Arrived at job site; (4) Job status update (during work); (5) Found additional issue (needs more work/cost); (6) Job complete/ready for walkthrough; (7) Follow-up after job (thank you + review request); (8) Running behind schedule; (9) Need to reschedule; (10) Parts on order/waiting for materials.
[Style] Professional but friendly—like a trusted contractor, not corporate.
[Parameters] Keep texts under 160 characters where possible. Include [CUSTOMIZATION BRACKETS]. Business name: [Your company name].
[Constraints] Quick to send (I'm on job sites). Clear and professional. Sets expectations properly. Builds trust through communication.
Difficult Situation ResponseCRISPE
[Capacity] You are a customer service advisor helping a trades professional handle a difficult customer situation.
[Role] Communication coach for service businesses.
[Insight] I have a customer issue: [complaint about work / refuses to pay / unreasonable request / warranty dispute / etc.]. I want to handle this professionally, protect my reputation, and get a fair resolution.
[Statement] Help me handle this situation: (1) Assess the Situation - is complaint valid, what's my liability, fair resolution; (2) Recommended Response - draft message/script, key points, what to avoid saying; (3) Documentation - what to document, photos/records to keep, CYA considerations; (4) Resolution Options - if I'm at fault, if I'm not at fault, compromise options.
[Personality] Professional, firm but fair, protective of reputation.
[Experiment] Give me a version for if I want to keep them as a customer and one for if I'm willing to part ways.
[The Situation] What happened: [Describe the issue]. Customer's claim: [What are they saying]. The facts: [What actually happened]. Documentation: [What do you have in writing].
[Constraints] Protect my reputation (they might leave reviews). Document everything going forward. Be professional regardless of how they act. Know when to involve lawyer/insurance.

Marketing & Reviews

Get more calls from better customers.

Review Response TemplatesCRISP
[Context] I'm a [trade professional] who needs to respond to online reviews professionally. Good responses help my business; bad responses hurt it.
[Request] Write a response to this [Google/Yelp/Facebook] review.
[Instructions] Review type: [5-star / 4-star / 3-star / negative / unfair]. Review text: "[Paste the review]". What actually happened: [Your side of the story]. For positive: thank specifically, mention what you did, invite them back. For negative: don't get defensive, acknowledge experience, offer to make it right, take offline if needed, show future readers you're professional.
[Style] Professional, genuine, brief.
[Parameters] Include business name (for SEO). Keep under 100 words for positive, under 150 for negative. Never argue publicly.
[Constraints] Potential customers are reading this. Don't reveal private job details. Stay professional even if they're wrong. Offer solution, not excuses.
Local Service Page ContentCOSTAR
[Context] You're helping a [trade] business create website content that ranks for local searches and converts visitors into calls.
[Objective] Write service page content for [specific service] in [city/area] that ranks in local search and makes people call.
[Style] Clear, trustworthy, locally relevant.
[Tone] Professional, knowledgeable, approachable.
[Audience] Local homeowners/businesses searching for this service.
[Response] Create service page content including: (1) Headline - service + location focused, clear value proposition; (2) Opening paragraph - what you do, area you serve, why choose you; (3) Services Detailed - specific services under this category, what's included, common problems you solve; (4) Why Choose Us - experience and credentials, guarantees/warranties, what makes you different; (5) Service Area - specific cities/neighborhoods, response times; (6) Call to Action - phone number prominent, easy estimate request; (7) FAQ Section - common questions, pricing expectations, what to expect.
[Business Details] Service: [What specific service]. Service area: [Cities/neighborhoods]. Experience: [Years, certifications, etc.]. Differentiators: [What makes you stand out]. Target keywords: [What do customers search for].
[Constraints] Write for humans first, search engines second. Include location naturally (not stuffed). Focus on customer outcomes. Include trust signals (licensed, insured, etc.). Clear call to action.

Trades Business Principles

AI for the Working Professional
  • Document everything. Use AI to help write up issues found, work done, and customer agreements.
  • Professionalism wins jobs. Clean estimates and clear communication beat the "guy who just gives a number."
  • Reviews are gold. AI helps you ask for reviews and respond professionally. Make this a habit.
  • Protect yourself. Clear agreements and good documentation prevent disputes.
  • Stay competitive. Marketing isn't optional anymore. AI makes it manageable.

Your Skills + Professional Image = Higher Rates

The best tradespeople often get beat by competitors with better marketing and communication. Your work speaks for itself, but only after they hire you. Use AI to present as professionally as the big companies while delivering the quality only a skilled craftsperson can.
