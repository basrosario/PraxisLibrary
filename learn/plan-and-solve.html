Planning Technique

Plan-and-Solve Prompting

Think before you act. Devise a plan, then execute it methodically for complex problem-solving.

The Method

Planning Before Execution

Plan-and-Solve prompting explicitly separates planning from execution. First, the model devises a strategy for solving the problem. Then, it follows the plan step by step. This prevents the model from diving in without thinking.

The Trigger Phrase

"Let's first understand the problem and devise a plan to solve it. Then, let's carry out the plan and solve the problem step by step."

Two-Phase Approach

First devise a plan, then execute it step by step. This separation improves reasoning quality.

Template

Plan-and-Solve Prompt Structure

The two key phases and how to trigger them

Phase 1: Planning

"First, understand the problem: What are we given? What do we need to find? What approach should we use?"

Strategy Development

Phase 2: Execution

"Now, carry out the plan step by step. Extract relevant variables, perform calculations, and verify the answer."

Systematic Solving
PS+ Enhancement

Plan-and-Solve+ (PS+) adds more detailed instructions: "Extract relevant variables and their values. Calculate intermediate results. Check calculations for accuracy." This reduces calculation errors.

Comparison

Plan-and-Solve vs. Standard CoT

See how explicit planning improves problem-solving

Standard CoT May Miss Steps
Prompt: "A store has 45 apples. They sold 1/3 in the morning and 1/5 of the remaining in the afternoon. How many are left? Let's think step by step."
CoT Response:
1/3 of 45 = 15 sold
45 - 15 = 30 remaining
1/5 of 45 = 9 sold...
(Error: used original amount instead of remaining)
Jumped into calculation without clear plan
Plan-and-Solve Structured
Prompt: "...Let's first understand and devise a plan..."
Plan: 1) Find morning sales 2) Calculate remaining 3) Find afternoon sales from remaining 4) Calculate final amount

Execute:
Morning: 45 × 1/3 = 15 sold
After morning: 45 - 15 = 30
Afternoon: 30 × 1/5 = 6 sold
Final: 30 - 6 = 24 apples
Plan prevents variable confusion
Standard CoT Unstructured
Problem: "Who is taller: Person A is shorter than B, B is shorter than C, D is taller than A but shorter than B."
CoT Response:
A is shorter than B... D is taller than A... so D might be between A and B... let me think... (loses track of relationships)
No systematic approach to ordering
Plan-and-Solve Systematic
With Plan-and-Solve:
Plan: 1) List all constraints 2) Build ordering from constraints 3) Place each person in sequence

Execute:
Constraints: A<B, B<C, A<D<B
Order: A < D < B < C
C is tallest
Plan creates clear methodology
Applications

When Plan-and-Solve Excels

Multi-Step Math

Word problems requiring multiple operations where variables need careful tracking.

Constraint Satisfaction

Problems with multiple conditions that must all be satisfied simultaneously.

Project Planning

Breaking down complex projects into phases with dependencies.

Algorithm Design

Creating step-by-step procedures before writing actual code.
