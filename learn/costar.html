The COSTAR Method

A comprehensive framework that emphasizes audience awareness and output format. Ideal for professional content creation.

Format of the output

Why COSTAR?

The framework that puts your audience first

COSTAR excels when audience matters. Unlike CRISP, which focuses on the task, COSTAR ensures your output is tailored to who will receive it.

Professional Communications

Emails, reports, and presentations that need to connect with specific recipients.

Marketing & Sales

Content that must persuade and resonate with target demographics.

User Documentation

Guides and help content written for specific user groups.

Published Content

Blog posts, articles, and content that will be shared publicly.

COSTAR vs. CRISP

CRISP uses "Parameters" for output constraints. COSTAR splits this into "Audience" and "Response" for more nuanced control. Choose COSTAR when the recipient significantly affects how you should write.

C - Context

Provide the background the AI needs to understand your situation

Include relevant details about your business, project, or circumstances. The more context you provide, the more tailored and relevant the output will be.

Strong Context Example

"I'm organizing our neighborhood's annual summer potluck. Last year we had some issues with too many desserts and not enough main dishes. This year I want to coordinate better so we have a balanced meal."

O - Objective

State your goal clearly and the action you want

What outcome do you want from this content? What should the reader think, feel, or do after engaging with it?

Inform

"Help readers understand the new policy changes"

Persuade

"Encourage neighbors to bring a variety of dishes"

Instruct

"Guide users through the onboarding process"

Engage

"Build excitement about our upcoming product launch"

S - Style

Define the writing approach and voice

Style refers to the writing approach. You can reference known styles or describe the characteristics you want.

Reference Styles

"Write like The Economist" or "In the style of Harvard Business Review"

Characteristic Styles

"Concise and data-driven" or "Narrative and story-based"

T - Tone

The emotional quality of your writing

Tone is how your content "feels" to the reader. Consider the spectrum of options for your context.

Formal
Casual
Serious
Playful
Reserved
Enthusiastic
Authoritative
Collaborative
Tone Example

"Use an empathetic and reassuring tone. We're delivering news about service changes, so acknowledge potential concerns while focusing on benefits."

A - Audience

Who will read this? Their characteristics shape everything.

Define your audience in detail. The better you describe them, the more the AI can tailor content to their needs, expectations, and level of understanding.

Knowledge Level

Technical experts vs. general audience

Role/Position

Executives vs. end users vs. developers

Relationship

Existing customers vs. prospects vs. partners

Concerns

What questions or objections might they have?

Audience Definition Example

"The audience is my neighbors - a mix of families with kids, retirees, and young professionals. They're busy people who want easy recipes. Some have dietary restrictions we need to consider."

R - Response

Specify the format and structure of your output

Define exactly what form the output should take. Be specific about format, length, and required elements.

Format

Email, report, blog post, script, outline

Length

Word count, page count, time to read

Structure

Headers, bullet points, numbered lists

Elements

Include summary, CTA, examples, data

Complete COSTAR Example

See all six components working together

C - Context

I'm organizing our neighborhood's annual summer potluck in the park. Last year we had too many desserts and not enough main dishes. We have about 30 households participating and need to coordinate who brings what.

O - Objective

Create an invitation that gets neighbors excited about the event, explains the dish sign-up system, and encourages a variety of food categories so we have a balanced meal.

S - Style

Warm and neighborly, like a friendly note from someone on the block. Keep it conversational but organized so the key details are easy to find.

T - Tone

Enthusiastic and welcoming. Make people feel included and excited to participate, while gently nudging them toward bringing main dishes or sides.

A - Audience

Our neighbors - a mix of families with young kids, retirees, and busy professionals. They want easy-to-follow details. Some are new to the neighborhood and may not know everyone yet.

R - Response

Invitation format, 150-200 words. Include event date/time/location, the sign-up categories (main dish, side, dessert, drinks), and how to RSVP. End with a friendly note about meeting new neighbors.
