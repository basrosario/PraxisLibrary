Example Selection

The right examples teach more than the right words. Choose demonstrations that maximize AI understanding.

The Challenge

Not All Examples Are Equal

Research shows that example selection can swing few-shot accuracy by over 20 percentage points. The difference between good and bad examples often matters more than the number of examples.

The Core Insight

Examples that are semantically similar to your query outperform random examples. The model learns from the pattern match between example inputs and your actual input.

Quality Over Quantity

Well-chosen examples consistently outperform poorly chosen ones. The right selection strategy matters more than adding more examples.

Strategies

How to Choose Examples

Four proven approaches ranked by effectiveness

1. Semantic Similarity

Choose examples whose inputs are semantically similar to your query. If classifying a product review, use reviews about similar products or with similar language patterns.

Most Effective

2. Diversity Coverage

Cover different categories, edge cases, and variations. If your task has 5 categories, include at least one example from each to show the model the full output space.

High Effectiveness

3. Complexity Matching

Match example complexity to your query. Simple queries benefit from simple examples; complex queries need examples that demonstrate thorough reasoning.

Medium Effectiveness

4. Boundary Examples

Include examples near decision boundaries. For sentiment analysis, show clearly positive, clearly negative, AND borderline cases to teach nuance.

Quick Reference

Example Selection Checklist

Relevance Check

Are examples from the same domain as your query? Tech examples for tech tasks, legal for legal, etc.

Clarity Check

Is each example unambiguous? Could a human confidently label it the same way?

Coverage Check

Do examples cover all expected output categories? Include at least one per class.

Balance Check

Are examples roughly balanced across categories? Avoid overrepresenting one class.

Avoid These

Common Selection Mistakes

Problem

If all your examples are clear-cut cases, the model won't learn to handle ambiguous inputs. "I LOVE this!" is easy to classify; "It's fine, I guess" teaches more.

Solution

Include 1-2 examples near decision boundaries. Show how edge cases should be handled.

Problem

Models may latch onto surface features. If all your positive examples contain "amazing," it might learn that word = positive, missing the actual pattern.

Solution

Vary vocabulary and phrasing across examples. Use synonyms and different sentence structures.

Problem

Research shows recency bias—examples at the end of your prompt have more influence than those at the beginning.

Solution

Place your most representative example last. If one example best matches your query, put it closest to the query.

Problem

More isn't always better. After 5-10 examples, returns diminish and you waste context window. Quality beats quantity.

Solution

Start with 3-5 well-chosen examples. Only add more if performance requires it. Test with fewer before adding more.
