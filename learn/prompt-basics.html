Core Concepts

The Four Essentials

Every effective prompt includes some combination of these elements

1

Clear Task

What do you want?

Start with a specific action verb. The AI needs to know exactly what you want it to do: write, analyze, explain, compare, summarize, create, or transform.

Vague "Something about marketing"
Clear "Write a 3-paragraph introduction for..."
2

Relevant Context

Set the scene

AI has vast knowledge but no knowledge of your situation. Fill in the who, what, where, when, and why so responses fit your actual needs.

Who You Are "I'm a project manager at a tech startup..."
Your Goal "We're preparing for a product launch in 2 weeks..."
3

Specifics

Define the output

Control format, length, style, and tone. Without these details, AI uses its own judgment. Constraints often improve quality by focusing the response.

Format "Bullet points with bold headers..."
Constraints "Under 200 words, professional tone..."
4

Examples (Optional)

Show, don't tell

When words aren't enough, show what you want. Examples help AI understand format, style, and quality expectations better than any description.

Sample Input "Here's an example of what I'm looking for..."
Anti-Example "Avoid outputs like this: [example]"

Key Skill

The Iteration Mindset

Great prompts are built, not written. Treat AI as a conversation, not a one-shot request.

1

Start Simple

Begin with a basic prompt to see what AI produces

2

Analyze

What's good? What's missing? What's wrong?

3

Refine

Add constraints, context, or examples to address gaps

4

Repeat

Continue until you get the results you need

Perfection Isn't the Goal

Don't expect perfect results on the first try. The best prompt engineers iterate quickly, learning from each response to craft better follow-ups. A great final result often takes 2-3 exchanges.

Flexibility

Labels vs Natural Language

Both approaches work equally well - use what feels natural to you

With Labels

Context: I'm a project manager asking a colleague to cover a client meeting while I'm on PTO.

Task: Write an email requesting they attend the meeting in my place.

Tone: Professional but friendly, since we work closely together.

Format: Brief email with meeting details and key talking points.

Great for learning Easy to double-check
OR
Natural Language

Help me write an email to my colleague Sarah asking if she can cover a client meeting for me next Tuesday while I'm on PTO. We work closely together so keep it friendly but professional. Include the meeting details (Tuesday 2pm with Acme Corp, project status update) and the 3 key points I'd normally cover. Keep it brief.

Feels conversational Faster to write

Both Get the Same Results

The AI understands both formats equally well. Labels can help you remember to include key elements when learning. Natural conversation flows faster once you've internalized the principles.

Key Principles to Remember

Evidence-based guidance for effective prompting

Specificity Wins

Prompts with specific parameters (word count, audience, format) produce outputs requiring 67% fewer revisions.

Context is Critical

Including "who, what, why, and for whom" improves output relevance by up to 43%.

Avoid Overloading

Multiple unrelated tasks in one prompt degrades quality. Break complex requests into focused steps.

Examples Accelerate

Few-shot prompting (1-3 examples) helps AI understand expectations and produces more consistent outputs.
