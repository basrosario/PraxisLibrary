Using AI Guide

Purpose: Comprehensive AI Education Audience: Everyone Level: Beginner to Advanced

A complete guide to understanding, choosing, and effectively using AI tools. Whether you're just getting started or looking to optimize your AI workflow, this guide covers everything you need to know.

AI is a Tool, Not Magic

The most effective AI users understand that AI amplifies human capability - it doesn't replace human judgment, creativity, or expertise. This guide will help you become a skilled AI collaborator.

What is AI?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to computer systems designed to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence - like understanding language, recognizing patterns, making decisions, and generating content.

How Modern AI Works

Today's AI systems learn from massive amounts of data to recognize patterns and make predictions. Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT and Claude are trained on text from books, websites, and documents to understand and generate human-like language.

What AI Can Do

AI excels at: summarizing information, generating drafts, answering questions, translating languages, analyzing data, writing code, creating images, and assisting with creative tasks. It's like having a knowledgeable assistant available 24/7.

What AI Cannot Do

AI cannot: truly understand meaning, guarantee accuracy, access real-time information (unless connected to the internet), replace human judgment, or take responsibility for decisions. Always verify important information.

The Human-AI Partnership

The best results come from combining human expertise with AI capability. You bring creativity, judgment, and context; AI brings speed, consistency, and the ability to process large amounts of information quickly.

Types of AI Tools

Different AI tools are designed for different tasks. Understanding what each type does best helps you choose the right tool for your needs.

Large Language Models (LLMs)

What they do: Understand and generate text, answer questions, write content, summarize documents, translate languages, help with analysis and reasoning.

Examples: ChatGPT (OpenAI), Claude (Anthropic), Gemini (Google), Llama (Meta), Copilot (Microsoft)

Best for: Writing, research, brainstorming, coding help, explanations, document analysis, general assistance

Image Generators

What they do: Create images from text descriptions, edit existing images, generate variations, create artwork and designs.

Examples: DALL-E (OpenAI), Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, Adobe Firefly, Ideogram

Best for: Concept art, illustrations, marketing visuals, prototyping designs, creative exploration

Code Assistants

What they do: Help write, debug, and explain code. Suggest completions, find errors, generate functions, and explain complex code.

Examples: GitHub Copilot, Claude Code, Cursor, Codeium, Amazon CodeWhisperer

Best for: Software development, debugging, learning to code, automating repetitive coding tasks

Voice Assistants

What they do: Respond to voice commands, control smart devices, set reminders, answer quick questions, play media.

Examples: Siri (Apple), Alexa (Amazon), Google Assistant, Cortana (Microsoft)

Best for: Hands-free tasks, quick information, smart home control, accessibility

AI Search & Research

What they do: Search the web with AI-powered summaries, find and synthesize information, provide sourced answers.

Examples: Perplexity AI, Google AI Overview, Bing Chat, You.com

Best for: Research, fact-checking, finding current information, exploring topics with sources

Specialized AI Tools

What they do: Focused tools for specific tasks like video creation, music generation, presentation design, data analysis.

Examples: Runway (video), Suno (music), Gamma (presentations), Julius (data analysis)

Best for: Specific creative or analytical tasks where specialized tools outperform general AI

How to Use AI Effectively

Getting great results from AI is a skill. Here are the key principles for effective AI use.

1. Be Specific

Vague prompts get vague results. Instead of "Write about marketing," try "Write a 200-word LinkedIn post about email marketing best practices for small business owners, with a friendly but professional tone."

2. Provide Context

AI doesn't know your situation unless you tell it. Share relevant background: your role, your audience, your goals, any constraints. The more context, the better the output.

3. Iterate and Refine

Rarely is the first output perfect. Treat AI interaction as a conversation. Ask for revisions, provide feedback, and refine until you get what you need.

4. Verify Important Information

AI can confidently state incorrect information (called "hallucinations"). Always verify facts, statistics, citations, and technical details from authoritative sources.

5. Use the Right Tool

Don't force a general LLM to do what a specialized tool does better. Use image generators for images, code assistants for code, and search tools for current information.

6. Think in Drafts

Use AI to generate first drafts quickly, then apply your expertise to refine. AI is excellent at getting you 80% of the way there fast; your skills finish the last 20%.

Structured Prompt Methods

Using structured prompt frameworks dramatically improves AI output quality. Here are the four methods used throughout this library.

CRISP

Clear & Structured

Components:

  • Context - Background information
  • Request - What you're asking for
  • Instructions - Specific guidelines
  • Style - Tone and format
  • Parameters - Constraints and requirements

Best for: Quick, focused tasks with clear requirements

CRISPE

Extended CRISP

Components:

  • Capacity - AI's role/expertise
  • Role - Specific persona
  • Insight - Your situation/needs
  • Statement - The actual request
  • Personality - Tone/approach
  • Experiment - Room for creativity

Best for: Complex tasks needing specific expertise

COSTAR

Comprehensive

Components:

  • Context - Situation background
  • Objective - Goal to achieve
  • Style - Writing style
  • Tone - Emotional quality
  • Audience - Who will read it
  • Response - Desired format

Best for: Content creation with specific audience

ReAct

Reasoning + Acting

Components:

  • Thought - Reasoning about the problem
  • Action - Steps to take
  • Observation - What was learned
  • Repeat - Iterate until solved

Best for: Problem-solving, research, multi-step tasks

Try It Yourself

Copy these example prompts and paste them into any AI tool to see structured prompting in action.

CRISP Example CRISP
Context: I'm a marketing manager at a small tech startup launching a new productivity app. Request: Write a product announcement email for our existing customers. Instructions: Include the key features, benefits, and a clear call-to-action. Keep it under 200 words. Style: Professional but friendly, enthusiastic without being pushy. Parameters: Subject line included, no jargon, mobile-friendly formatting.
COSTAR Example COSTAR
Context: Our company just achieved carbon-neutral certification after a 3-year sustainability initiative. Objective: Create a LinkedIn post announcing this achievement. Style: Inspiring and authentic, avoiding corporate buzzwords. Tone: Proud but humble, grateful to employees and partners. Audience: Business professionals, potential employees, industry peers. Response: 150-200 word post with 3-5 relevant hashtags.
ReAct Example ReAct
I need to plan a team-building event for 15 people with a $500 budget. Use the ReAct approach: Thought: First, consider what types of activities work for groups of 15 within budget constraints. Action: List 5 activity options with estimated costs. Observation: Evaluate which options best balance engagement, cost, and logistics. Then repeat this process to narrow down to a final recommendation with a complete plan.

AI Safety & Ethics

Responsible AI use protects you, your organization, and others. Here's what you need to know.

Privacy & Confidentiality

Never input sensitive personal information, trade secrets, or confidential data into AI tools unless you have explicit authorization and understand the tool's data policies. Most AI providers may use inputs for training.

Verify Before Trusting

AI can generate plausible-sounding but incorrect information. Always fact-check important claims, verify citations exist, and validate technical information before using it in important work.

Bias Awareness

AI systems can reflect biases present in their training data. Be aware that outputs may contain subtle biases related to gender, race, culture, or other factors. Review outputs critically.

Transparency & Attribution

When appropriate, be transparent about AI assistance. Many contexts (academic, professional, creative) have evolving norms about AI disclosure. Know your organization's policies.

Human Oversight

AI should augment human decision-making, not replace it entirely. Maintain human oversight for important decisions, especially those affecting people's lives, safety, or rights.

Stay Informed

AI capabilities and best practices evolve rapidly. Stay current on developments, new tools, and emerging ethical considerations. What's true today may change tomorrow.

When NOT to Use AI: Avoid using AI for: making final medical/legal/financial decisions, generating content claiming to be from real people, creating deepfakes or deceptive content, accessing systems without authorization, or any task where errors could cause significant harm.
