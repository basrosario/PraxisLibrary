Mid-Size Business Prompts

Size: 100-999 Employees Focus: Scaling Smart Challenge: Growing Pains

Mid-size businesses have outgrown startup chaos but don't have enterprise resources. You need systems and processes that scale, efficiency gains that compound, and the ability to punch above your weight. AI is your force multiplier.

The Scale-Up Sweet Spot

You're big enough to have real impact from AI efficiency gains, but nimble enough to implement quickly. This is where AI ROI is often highest.

Operations & Scaling

Build processes that grow with you.

Process Documentation Builder COSTAR
Context: You are helping a growing business document their processes so they can be trained consistently and scaled effectively.

Objective: Create comprehensive process documentation that enables consistent execution and easy onboarding of new team members.

Style: Clear, step-by-step documentation with visual elements where helpful.

Tone: Professional but accessible, assuming someone new to the role is reading.

Audience: New employees, team members covering for each other, future process improvers.

Response: Create a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) for [process name]: 1. **Process Overview** - Purpose: Why does this process exist? - Owner: Who is responsible? - Frequency: When/how often is this done? - Inputs: What do you need to start? - Outputs: What should you have when done? 2. **Step-by-Step Instructions** - Numbered steps with clear actions - Decision points (if X, then Y) - Screenshots or examples where helpful - Common mistakes to avoid 3. **Tools & Access Required** - Systems needed - Login/access requirements - Where to find resources 4. **Quality Checkpoints** - How to verify you did it right - Common errors to check for - When to escalate 5. **Troubleshooting** - Common problems and solutions - Who to contact for help - FAQs 6. **Revision History** - Version number - Last updated - Changes from previous version [Process to Document] Process name: [What is this process?] Current state: [Describe how it works today—even if messy] Who does it: [Role/team] Tools used: [Systems, software, etc.] Known issues: [What goes wrong?] [Constraints] - Write for someone with no prior knowledge - Include actual examples where possible - Flag steps that frequently cause confusion - Make it searchable and scannable
Bottleneck Identifier CRISPE
Capacity: You are an operations consultant specializing in helping growing businesses identify and eliminate bottlenecks.

Role: Process improvement analyst for scale-up businesses.

Insight: Our team is struggling to keep up with demand. Work is backing up and we're not sure where the real bottleneck is. I'll describe our workflow and the symptoms we're seeing.

Statement: Analyze our workflow and help us identify: 1. **Bottleneck Analysis** - Where is work accumulating? - What's the constraint limiting throughput? - Is this a capacity, capability, or process issue? 2. **Root Cause Investigation** - Why does this bottleneck exist? - What created it (growth, process, people, tools)? - Is this the real bottleneck or a symptom? 3. **Quick Relief Options** - What can we do this week to reduce pressure? - Temporary workarounds - Quick process tweaks 4. **Systemic Solutions** - What would permanently fix this? - Process redesign options - Tool/automation opportunities - Capacity/capability investments needed 5. **Prevention** - How do we avoid this bottleneck as we grow? - Early warning indicators to monitor - Scalability considerations

Personality: Practical, focused on quick wins and sustainable solutions.

Experiment: Apply Theory of Constraints thinking—if we fix this bottleneck, where will the new one appear? [Workflow Description] Process: [Describe the workflow end-to-end] Symptoms: [What are you experiencing?] Volume: [How much work, what's changed?] Team: [Who's involved, what's their capacity?] Tools: [What systems are used?] [Constraints] - Solutions must be realistic for our size - Consider both immediate relief and long-term fixes - Account for the human element (not just process) - Help us prioritize what to fix first

Role Definition Developer COSTAR
Context: You are an HR consultant helping a growing company define roles clearly as they scale and specialize.

Objective: Create clear role definitions that help with hiring, performance management, and career development.

Style: Professional HR documentation that's also useful for actual humans.

Tone: Clear, specific, motivating—not corporate jargon.

Audience: Hiring managers, candidates, current role holders, HR.

Response: Create a comprehensive role definition for [position]: 1. **Role Summary** - One-paragraph description of what this role does and why it matters - Where it fits in the organization 2. **Key Responsibilities** (5-8 items) - Primary duties with clear outcomes - Percentage of time allocation - What success looks like for each 3. **Required Skills & Experience** - Must-have qualifications - Nice-to-have qualifications - Experience level expected 4. **Success Metrics** - How we measure performance in this role - Key results expected (quarterly/annually) - Quality indicators 5. **Growth Path** - What this role grows into - Skills to develop for advancement - Typical timeline 6. **Working Relationships** - Reports to - Direct reports (if any) - Key collaborators - External relationships 7. **Compensation Context** - Level in the organization - Market positioning [Role Context] Position: [Title] Department: [Where does this sit?] Current state: [New role or existing?] Why needed: [What problem does this solve?] Similar roles: [What exists that's comparable?] [Constraints] - Be specific enough to evaluate candidates - Be flexible enough to allow growth - Focus on outcomes, not just activities - Avoid gendered language - Make it realistic—not a unicorn hunt
Onboarding Program Designer CRISPE
Capacity: You are an organizational development specialist creating onboarding programs that accelerate new hire productivity.

Role: Employee experience designer for growing companies.

Insight: We're hiring more people and our onboarding is ad-hoc. New hires take too long to become productive and we're losing some early. I need a structured onboarding program.

Statement: Design an onboarding program for [role type] that covers: **Week 1: Foundation** - Day 1 experience (first impressions matter) - Essential setup (tools, access, workspace) - Key relationship introductions - Company context and culture **Week 2-4: Learning** - Role-specific training curriculum - Shadowing and observation schedule - Early quick wins to build confidence - Check-in cadence **Month 2-3: Contributing** - Gradual responsibility increase - Mentorship/buddy program - Feedback loops - First real projects **90-Day Checkpoint** - Assessment criteria - Conversation guide - Decision point framework - Continued development plan For each phase, include: - Specific activities and timeline - Resources needed - Who's responsible - Success indicators

Personality: Practical, employee-centric, focused on time-to-productivity.

Experiment: Include a "new hire feedback survey" to continuously improve the program. [Company Context] Company size: [Number of employees] Role type: [What role is this for?] Current onboarding: [What do we do now?] Common new hire struggles: [What goes wrong?] Culture: [What should new hires understand about how we work?] [Constraints] - Program must be manageable for managers - Include remote/hybrid considerations - Build in flexibility for different learning speeds - Focus on relationship building, not just information transfer

Scale revenue without scaling chaos.

Sales Process Optimizer COSTAR
Context: You are a sales operations consultant helping a growing company build a scalable, repeatable sales process.

Objective: Analyze our current sales approach and help us build a process that can scale with more reps.

Style: Practical sales methodology with clear stages and actions.

Tone: Results-focused, realistic, seller-friendly.

Audience: Sales leadership, sales reps, revenue operations.

Response: Based on our current approach, help us define: 1. **Sales Process Stages** - Stage name and definition - Entry criteria (what must be true to be here) - Exit criteria (what must happen to advance) - Key activities per stage - Typical timeframe 2. **Qualification Framework** - What makes a qualified opportunity? - Disqualification criteria (when to walk away) - Required information at each stage 3. **Sales Playbook Elements** - Key questions to ask - Common objections and responses - Competitive positioning - Value propositions by persona 4. **Metrics & Reporting** - Stage conversion rates to track - Velocity metrics - Leading indicators - Rep performance measures 5. **Tool & Technology Recommendations** - CRM configuration - Sales enablement needs - Automation opportunities [Current Sales Context] Product/Service: [What we sell] Target customer: [Who we sell to] Average deal size: [Typical contract value] Current process: [How sales works today] Pain points: [What's not working?] Sales team size: [Number of reps] [Constraints] - Process must work for our deal complexity - Keep it simple enough that reps will follow it - Build in coaching touchpoints - Focus on buyer journey, not just our process
Customer Success Framework CRISPE
Capacity: You are a customer success strategist helping companies retain and grow their customer base.

Role: Customer success program designer for B2B companies.

Insight: We've grown our customer base but our retention and expansion could be better. We need a systematic approach to customer success, not just reactive support.

Statement: Help us build a customer success framework: 1. **Customer Journey Mapping** - Key lifecycle stages - Critical moments that matter - Risk signals at each stage - Expansion opportunities 2. **Success Metrics Definition** - Health score components - Leading vs. lagging indicators - At-risk triggers - Expansion readiness signals 3. **Engagement Model** - Touch cadence by segment (high/medium/low touch) - QBR structure and content - Proactive outreach triggers - Escalation paths 4. **Playbooks** - Onboarding playbook - Adoption playbook - At-risk intervention playbook - Expansion playbook - Renewal playbook 5. **Team Structure Recommendations** - CSM to customer ratios by segment - Role responsibilities - Handoff processes (sales to CS, CS to support)

Personality: Customer-obsessed, data-driven, practical.

Experiment: Design a "customer advisory board" structure to deepen relationships with top accounts. [Customer Context] Business model: [SaaS, services, etc.] Customer segments: [How do you segment customers?] Current retention: [What's your retention/churn rate?] Team size: [CS team resources] Pain points: [What's not working?] [Constraints] - Must be scalable with limited CS resources - Balance high-touch and tech-touch approaches - Focus on outcomes, not just activities - Include customer perspective, not just our goals

Scale-Up Principles

Building for Scale
  • Document before you forget. The process in your head needs to be on paper before you hire the next person.
  • Automate the repetitive. If you do it more than twice a week, find a way to automate or templatize it.
  • Hire ahead of the curve. By the time you desperately need someone, you're already behind.
  • Communicate more than you think you need to. As you grow, information doesn't travel automatically.
  • Build systems, not dependencies. If only one person can do it, you have a risk, not a process.
  • Measure what matters. But don't measure everything—focus on the metrics that drive decisions.

The Messy Middle Is Where Winners Are Made

Scaling a mid-size business is harder than starting a company or running an enterprise. You have the complexity without the resources. AI helps you punch above your weight—use it to build the infrastructure that will support your next stage of growth.
