Startup & Solo Prompts

Size: 1-9 People Focus: Multiply Yourself Challenge: Everything, All at Once

When you're building from scratch—whether it's a funded startup or a solo side hustle—you don't have teams. You ARE the team. AI is the closest thing to cloning yourself. These prompts help you do the work of ten people while you figure out what actually works.

The Unfair Advantage

Previous generations of entrepreneurs had to choose between doing it all badly or spending money they didn't have on help. You can have a marketing department, research team, and strategy consultant—all for the cost of an AI subscription.

Idea Validation & Research

Test before you build. Learn before you leap.

Business Idea Stress Tester COSTAR
Context: You are a veteran startup advisor who has seen thousands of ideas succeed and fail. You're helpful but brutally honest.

Objective: Stress test my business idea—poke holes in it, challenge my assumptions, and help me see what I might be missing.

Style: Direct, analytical, like a tough-but-fair investor meeting.

Tone: Honest, constructive—not mean, but not sugarcoated.

Audience: Me, an entrepreneur who would rather hear hard truths now than fail later.

Response: Analyze my idea through these lenses: 1. **Problem Validation** - Is this a real problem people have? - How do they solve it now? - How painful is the problem really? - Who has this problem and how many? 2. **Solution Analysis** - Does my solution actually solve the problem? - Is it 10x better than alternatives? - What are the assumptions I'm making? - What could prevent it from working? 3. **Market Reality** - Is the market big enough to matter? - Who are the competitors (including indirect)? - Why hasn't someone already done this? - What's my unfair advantage? 4. **Business Model** - Will people actually pay for this? - How much can I realistically charge? - What are the unit economics? - How do I acquire customers affordably? 5. **Execution Questions** - What are the hardest parts to build/do? - What do I need to be true to succeed? - What should I test first? - What would make this fail? 6. **Honest Assessment** - Strengths of this idea - Biggest concerns - What I'd want to see validated before investing time/money - Go/no-go recommendation [My Idea] The idea: [Describe your business idea] Target customer: [Who is it for?] The problem: [What problem does it solve?] The solution: [How does it solve it?] Why me: [Why am I the person to do this?] Current status: [Idea stage, prototype, launched?] [Constraints] - Be honest—I'd rather hear problems now - Don't just list problems, help me see how to address them - Challenge assumptions I might not realize I'm making - Consider both startup and solopreneur angles
Competitive Landscape Mapper CRISPE
Capacity: You are a market research analyst helping a founder understand their competitive landscape.

Role: Competitive intelligence researcher for early-stage ventures.

Insight: I'm entering [market/space] and need to understand who I'm up against—both direct competitors and alternative solutions people use.

Statement: Based on what I tell you about my business, help me map the competitive landscape: 1. **Direct Competitors** - Who solves the same problem for the same customer? - How do they position themselves? - What do they charge? - Strengths and weaknesses 2. **Indirect Competitors** - What other solutions do people use? - DIY/manual alternatives - Adjacent products that could expand here 3. **Competitive Positioning Map** - Key differentiating dimensions in this market - Where competitors sit on these dimensions - White space opportunities 4. **Competitive Advantages** - What would make me win against each? - What could they do that would crush me? - Sustainable vs. temporary advantages 5. **Learning Opportunities** - What can I learn from their reviews/feedback? - What are customers complaining about? - What features/approaches seem to work?

Personality: Thorough, strategic, focused on actionable insights.

Experiment: Identify one competitor I should watch closely and one I should ignore. [My Business] What I'm building: [Description] Target customer: [Who] Key differentiator: [Why I'm different] Competitors I know about: [List any you're aware of] [Constraints] - Base on publicly available information - Include both direct and indirect competition - Focus on insights I can act on - Help me find positioning opportunity, not just threats

Content & Marketing

Build an audience before you build a product.

Content Strategy for Solopreneurs COSTAR
Context: You are a content strategist helping a solopreneur build their personal brand and attract their ideal customers through valuable content.

Objective: Create a sustainable content strategy that I can actually execute without burning out—one that builds trust and generates business.

Style: Practical, sustainable, focused on quality over quantity.

Tone: Encouraging but realistic about solo capacity.

Audience: Me, one person trying to build a business and create content.

Response: Help me build a content strategy: 1. **Content Pillars** (3-5 topics) - What topics establish my expertise? - What does my audience care about? - What can I talk about consistently? 2. **Platform Strategy** - Which 1-2 platforms should I focus on? - Why these match my strengths and audience? - What format works best for each? 3. **Content Calendar** - Realistic posting frequency - Content types and mix - Batch creation approach 4. **Content Templates** - Repeatable formats I can use - "Fill in the blank" structures - Ways to repurpose content 5. **Engagement Strategy** - How to grow without spending all day on social - Community building approach - Converting followers to customers 6. **Sustainability Plan** - How to create without burnout - What to do when motivation is low - How to measure what's working [My Context] Business: [What I do/sell] Expertise: [What I know deeply] Target audience: [Who I want to reach] Current presence: [Where I am now] Time available: [Hours per week for content] Strengths: [Writing, video, speaking, etc.] [Constraints] - Must be doable by one person - Quality over quantity - Build towards business goals, not just followers - Include ways to repurpose and maximize each piece
LinkedIn Post Generator CRISP
Context: I'm a [your role/title] building my personal brand on LinkedIn. I want to share valuable insights that attract [target audience].

Request: Write a LinkedIn post about [topic/idea] that provides value, shows my expertise, and encourages engagement.

Instructions: - Start with a hook that stops the scroll - Share insight, story, or lesson (not just information) - Make it actionable—readers should learn something - End with engagement prompt - Format for easy reading (short paragraphs, line breaks)

Style: Conversational, authentic, thought-provoking—not preachy or salesy.

Parameters: - Length: [short/medium/long] (~100/200/300 words) - Include: [personal story, data, contrarian take, how-to, etc.] - Tone: [Inspirational, educational, provocative, vulnerable, etc.] [Topic Details] Main point: [What's the key message?] Supporting content: [Examples, data, story elements] Call to action: [What do you want readers to do?] [Constraints] - Sound like ME, not a corporate marketer - Provide real value, not fluff - No cringe humble-brags - Make it specific, not generic advice - Include something only I could share

Product & Development

Build the right thing, the right way.

MVP Scope Definer COSTAR
Context: You are a product strategist helping a founder define the minimum viable product—what to build first to test their idea with minimal investment.

Objective: Help me define an MVP that tests my core hypothesis without building more than necessary.

Style: Lean startup methodology, focused on learning over building.

Tone: Pragmatic, ruthless about scope, focused on validation.

Audience: Early-stage founder with limited time and resources.

Response: Help me scope an MVP: 1. **Core Hypothesis** - What are we really testing? - What must be true for this to work? - What's the riskiest assumption? 2. **Minimum Feature Set** - What's absolutely required to test the hypothesis? - What can we fake/manual/skip for v1? - What feels essential but isn't? 3. **Cut List** - Features that can wait - Nice-to-haves vs. must-haves - What to defer to v2, v3 4. **Build vs. Buy vs. Fake** - What needs to be built? - What can we use existing tools for? - What can be done manually behind the scenes? 5. **Success Criteria** - How do we know if MVP works? - What metrics prove/disprove the hypothesis? - What results trigger next phase? 6. **Timeline & Resources** - Realistic build estimate - What skills/resources needed - Biggest risks to timeline [My Product Idea] Product: [What are you building?] Problem: [What problem does it solve?] Initial feature list: [Everything I think I need] Resources: [Time, money, skills available] Goal: [What I want to learn/prove] [Constraints] - Be ruthless about scope - "If it doesn't test the hypothesis, cut it" - Consider no-code/low-code options - Think about manual processes that could work at small scale - Focus on learning, not impressing
User Interview Script Builder CRISPE
Capacity: You are a user research expert helping founders learn from potential customers without biasing their responses.

Role: Customer discovery coach using "The Mom Test" methodology.

Insight: I need to interview potential customers about [problem/solution] but I want to learn the truth, not just hear validation. Help me ask the right questions.

Statement: Create an interview script that: 1. **Opening** (build rapport) - How to introduce myself - How to frame the conversation - Set expectations 2. **Problem Discovery Questions** - Questions about their current situation - Questions about the problem (without naming it) - Questions about current solutions - Questions about pain level and frequency 3. **Solution Exploration** - How to present the idea (if at all) - Questions about potential value - Questions about willingness to pay/use - Questions that reveal true interest vs. politeness 4. **Closing** - Ask for referrals to others - Next steps if they're interested - Thank you and follow-up 5. **Things to Avoid** - Questions that bias responses - Hypothetical "would you" questions - Fishing for compliments - Talking more than listening

Personality: Curious, genuinely interested, comfortable with uncomfortable truths.

Experiment: Include "red flag" responses that suggest I should NOT build this, so I know what to watch for. [My Research Context] Product idea: [What I'm exploring] Target customer: [Who I want to interview] Core hypothesis: [What I'm trying to learn] What I'm afraid to hear: [What would disprove my idea] [Constraints] - Questions should uncover truth, not validation - Focus on past behavior, not future intentions - Ask about their life, not my solution - Make it conversational, not an interrogation

Quick Founder Prompts

Elevator Pitch Refiner

"Help me create a 30-second pitch for [my business/product]. Target: [audience]. It should explain the problem, solution, and why me—without jargon."

Landing Page Copy

"Write landing page copy for [product]. Above the fold: headline, subheadline, CTA. Below: benefits (not features), social proof section, FAQ, final CTA."

Cold Email Template

"Write a cold outreach email to [target: investors, potential customers, partners] about [purpose]. Keep it short, specific, and with a clear ask."

Pricing Brainstorm

"Help me think through pricing for [product/service]. Target customer: [who]. Competitors charge: [range]. What pricing models should I consider and why?"

Weekly Planning

"Help me plan my week. I have [X] hours. My top priorities are [list]. What should I focus on and what should I deliberately NOT do this week?"

Rejection Reframe

"I just got rejected by [investor/customer/partner] who said [feedback]. Help me learn from this: what's valid, what to ignore, and what to do differently."

The Founder's AI Mindset

Using AI Without Losing Your Edge
  • AI accelerates, it doesn't replace. You still need the vision, judgment, and hustle. AI just makes you faster.
  • Talk to real humans. AI can help you prepare for customer conversations—but it can't replace them.
  • Don't outsource thinking. Use AI to explore ideas and challenge assumptions, but the decisions are yours.
  • Ship imperfect things. AI can help you iterate faster, but don't let it become perfectionism in disguise.
  • Your unique perspective matters. AI knows averages. Your weird insight is your competitive advantage.
  • Stay scrappy. AI should save you money, not become a new overhead. Free and cheap tools often work fine.
