Idea Validation & Research

Test before you build. Learn before you leap.

Business Idea Stress TesterCOSTAR
[Context] You are a veteran startup advisor who has seen thousands of ideas succeed and fail. You're helpful but brutally honest.
[Objective] Stress test my business idea—poke holes in it, challenge my assumptions, and help me see what I might be missing.
[Style] Direct, analytical, like a tough-but-fair investor meeting.
[Tone] Honest, constructive—not mean, but not sugarcoated.
[Audience] Me, an entrepreneur who would rather hear hard truths now than fail later.
[Response] Analyze my idea through: (1) Problem Validation - is it real, how solved now, how painful, who/how many affected; (2) Solution Analysis - does it solve the problem, 10x better than alternatives, assumptions, what could fail; (3) Market Reality - big enough, competitors, why not done already, unfair advantage; (4) Business Model - will they pay, how much, unit economics, acquisition cost; (5) Execution Questions - hardest parts, what must be true, what to test first, failure modes; (6) Honest Assessment - strengths, concerns, validation needed, go/no-go recommendation.
[My Idea] The idea: [Describe]. Target customer: [Who]. Problem: [What it solves]. Solution: [How]. Why me: [Why I'm the one]. Status: [Idea/prototype/launched].
[Constraints] Be honest—I'd rather hear problems now. Don't just list problems, help address them. Challenge hidden assumptions. Consider both startup and solopreneur angles.
Competitive Landscape MapperCRISPE
[Context] You are a market research analyst helping a founder understand their competitive landscape.
[Role] Competitive intelligence researcher for early-stage ventures.
[Instruction] I'm entering [market/space] and need to understand who I'm up against. Map the competitive landscape: (1) Direct Competitors - same problem/customer, positioning, pricing, strengths/weaknesses; (2) Indirect Competitors - other solutions, DIY alternatives, adjacent products; (3) Positioning Map - differentiating dimensions, where competitors sit, white space; (4) Competitive Advantages - what makes me win, what could crush me, sustainable vs temporary; (5) Learning Opportunities - their reviews/feedback, customer complaints, working approaches.
[Style] Thorough, strategic, focused on actionable insights.
[Parameters] Identify one competitor I should watch closely and one I should ignore.
[Example] "Watch: Competitor X (growing fast, well-funded, similar positioning). Ignore: Competitor Y (enterprise-focused, different ICP, won't compete at your price point)."
[My Business] Building: [Description]. Target customer: [Who]. Differentiator: [Why different]. Known competitors: [List any].
[Constraints] Base on publicly available info. Include direct and indirect competition. Focus on actionable insights. Find positioning opportunity, not just threats.

Content & Marketing

Build an audience before you build a product.

Content Strategy for SolopreneursCOSTAR
[Context] You are a content strategist helping a solopreneur build their personal brand and attract customers through valuable content.
[Objective] Create a sustainable content strategy I can execute without burning out—one that builds trust and generates business.
[Style] Practical, sustainable, focused on quality over quantity.
[Tone] Encouraging but realistic about solo capacity.
[Audience] Me, one person trying to build a business and create content.
[Response] Build a content strategy: (1) Content Pillars (3-5) - topics that establish expertise, what audience cares about, consistent themes; (2) Platform Strategy - which 1-2 platforms, why they match strengths/audience, best formats; (3) Content Calendar - realistic frequency, content mix, batch approach; (4) Content Templates - repeatable formats, fill-in-blank structures, repurposing methods; (5) Engagement Strategy - grow without living on social, community building, converting to customers; (6) Sustainability Plan - create without burnout, low-motivation days, measuring success.
[My Context] Business: [What I do]. Expertise: [What I know]. Target audience: [Who to reach]. Current presence: [Where I am]. Time available: [Hours/week]. Strengths: [Writing/video/speaking].
[Constraints] Doable by one person. Quality over quantity. Build towards business goals. Include repurposing strategies.
LinkedIn Post GeneratorCRISP
[Context] I'm a [your role/title] building my personal brand on LinkedIn. I want to share valuable insights that attract [target audience].
[Role] LinkedIn content creator who writes posts that stop the scroll and build authority.
[Instruction] Write a LinkedIn post about [topic/idea]. Start with a scroll-stopping hook. Share insight/story/lesson (not just information). Make it actionable. End with engagement prompt. Format for easy reading (short paragraphs, line breaks).
[Style] Conversational, authentic, thought-provoking—not preachy or salesy.
[Parameters] Length: [short/medium/long] (~100/200/300 words). Include: [personal story, data, contrarian take, how-to]. Tone: [Inspirational, educational, provocative, vulnerable].
[Topic Details] Main point: [Key message]. Supporting content: [Examples, data, stories]. Call to action: [What readers should do].
[Constraints] Sound like ME, not a corporate marketer. Provide real value. No cringe humble-brags. Make it specific, not generic. Include something only I could share.
Newsletter WriterCRISPE
[Context] You are a newsletter writer who creates content that people actually open and read.
[Role] Email content creator for personal brand newsletters.
[Instruction] I write a [frequency] newsletter for [audience] about [topic]. Help me write this week's issue including: (1) Subject Line Options (5) - curiosity, benefit, question, direct, wildcard; (2) Opening Hook - grab attention, connect to reader, transition to content; (3) Main Content - educational/story/analysis with clear takeaways, scannable formatting; (4) Personal Touch - something only I'd share, behind-the-scenes, human moment; (5) Call to Action - what to do next, make it easy.
[Style] [Match my voice: Witty, thoughtful, direct, warm, etc.]
[Parameters] Include one unexpected element—controversial take, vulnerability, or surprising analogy.
[Example] "Subject: I almost quit yesterday (Here's why I didn't)" - curiosity + vulnerability hook.
[This Week's Content] Topic: [What it's about]. Key points: [What to convey]. Stories/examples: [Anecdotes]. Promotion: [Business mention].
[Constraints] Keep under [X] words. Sound like me. Deliver value without clicks. ONE call to action only. Make it worth their time.

Product & Development

Build the right thing, the right way.

MVP Scope DefinerCOSTAR
[Context] You are a product strategist helping a founder define the minimum viable product—what to build first to test their idea with minimal investment.
[Objective] Help me define an MVP that tests my core hypothesis without building more than necessary.
[Style] Lean startup methodology, focused on learning over building.
[Tone] Pragmatic, ruthless about scope, focused on validation.
[Audience] Early-stage founder with limited time and resources.
[Response] Help me scope an MVP: (1) Core Hypothesis - what we're testing, what must be true, riskiest assumption; (2) Minimum Feature Set - required to test hypothesis, what to fake/manual/skip, feels essential but isn't; (3) Cut List - features that wait, nice-to-haves vs must-haves, defer to v2/v3; (4) Build vs Buy vs Fake - what to build, existing tools, manual behind scenes; (5) Success Criteria - how to know it works, metrics to prove/disprove, what triggers next phase; (6) Timeline & Resources - realistic estimate, skills needed, timeline risks.
[My Product Idea] Product: [What you're building]. Problem: [What it solves]. Initial features: [Everything I think I need]. Resources: [Time, money, skills]. Goal: [What to learn/prove].
[Constraints] Be ruthless about scope. "If it doesn't test the hypothesis, cut it." Consider no-code/low-code. Think manual processes at small scale. Focus on learning, not impressing.
User Interview Script BuilderCRISPE
[Context] You are a user research expert helping founders learn from potential customers without biasing their responses.
[Role] Customer discovery coach using "The Mom Test" methodology.
[Instruction] I need to interview potential customers about [problem/solution] but want truth, not validation. Create an interview script: (1) Opening - how to introduce, frame conversation, set expectations; (2) Problem Discovery - current situation, problem without naming it, current solutions, pain level/frequency; (3) Solution Exploration - how to present idea (if at all), potential value, willingness to pay, true interest vs politeness; (4) Closing - ask for referrals, next steps, thank you; (5) Things to Avoid - biasing questions, hypothetical "would you" questions, fishing for compliments, talking more than listening.
[Style] Curious, genuinely interested, comfortable with uncomfortable truths.
[Parameters] Include "red flag" responses that suggest I should NOT build this, so I know what to watch for.
[Example] Red flag: "That sounds interesting" + no follow-up questions = polite disinterest. Red flag: "I'd probably use that" = hypothetical, not commitment.
[Research Context] Product idea: [What exploring]. Target customer: [Who to interview]. Core hypothesis: [What to learn]. Afraid to hear: [What disproves idea].
[Constraints] Uncover truth, not validation. Focus on past behavior, not future intentions. Ask about their life, not my solution. Conversational, not interrogation.

Quick Founder Prompts

Elevator Pitch Refiner

"Help me create a 30-second pitch for [my business/product]. Target: [audience]. It should explain the problem, solution, and why me—without jargon."

Landing Page Copy

"Write landing page copy for [product]. Above the fold: headline, subheadline, CTA. Below: benefits (not features), social proof section, FAQ, final CTA."

Cold Email Template

"Write a cold outreach email to [target: investors, potential customers, partners] about [purpose]. Keep it short, specific, and with a clear ask."

Pricing Brainstorm

"Help me think through pricing for [product/service]. Target customer: [who]. Competitors charge: [range]. What pricing models should I consider and why?"

Weekly Planning

"Help me plan my week. I have [X] hours. My top priorities are [list]. What should I focus on and what should I deliberately NOT do this week?"

Rejection Reframe

"I just got rejected by [investor/customer/partner] who said [feedback]. Help me learn from this: what's valid, what to ignore, and what to do differently."

The Founder's AI Mindset

Using AI Without Losing Your Edge
  • AI accelerates, it doesn't replace. You still need the vision, judgment, and hustle. AI just makes you faster.
  • Talk to real humans. AI can help you prepare for customer conversations—but it can't replace them.
  • Don't outsource thinking. Use AI to explore ideas and challenge assumptions, but the decisions are yours.
  • Ship imperfect things. AI can help you iterate faster, but don't let it become perfectionism in disguise.
  • Your unique perspective matters. AI knows averages. Your weird insight is your competitive advantage.
  • Stay scrappy. AI should save you money, not become a new overhead. Free and cheap tools often work fine.
