AI for Educators

Focus: Teaching Enhancement Approach: AI as Teaching Partner Goal: More Time for Students

These prompts are designed to help educators leverage AI as a powerful teaching assistant—not to replace your expertise, but to amplify it. Spend less time on administrative tasks and more time doing what you do best: inspiring students.

The AI-Assisted Educator

When you embrace AI as a teaching tool, you model the skills your students will need in their careers. You're not just teaching content—you're teaching adaptability.

Lesson Planning & Curriculum

Create engaging, differentiated lessons in a fraction of the time.

Differentiated Lesson Plan Generator COSTAR
Context: You are helping a [Grade Level] [Subject] teacher create a differentiated lesson plan.

Objective: Design a comprehensive lesson on [Topic] that addresses multiple learning levels and styles within the same classroom.

Style: Write as an experienced curriculum designer who understands Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles.

Tone: Practical, teacher-friendly, ready to implement.

Audience: A classroom with diverse learners including advanced students, on-level students, and students needing additional support.

Response: Provide a lesson plan that includes: 1. Learning objectives (tiered for different levels) 2. Hook/engagement activity 3. Direct instruction with built-in checkpoints 4. Three differentiated practice activities (challenge, on-level, supported) 5. Formative assessment strategies 6. Extension options for early finishers 7. Accommodations notes [Constraints] - Activities should be achievable within a [X]-minute class period - Include at least one collaborative and one independent activity - Suggest specific, free resources where applicable - All activities should connect to the same core learning objective
Unit Curriculum Mapper CRISPE
Capacity: You are a curriculum specialist with expertise in backward design and standards alignment.

Role: Curriculum development consultant for K-12 education.

Insight: I need to plan a [X]-week unit on [Topic] for [Grade Level] [Subject]. The unit should align with [State/Common Core] standards and build toward [End Assessment Type].

Statement: Create a unit map that shows the progression of learning, including: - Essential questions and enduring understandings - Week-by-week learning targets - Key vocabulary and concepts per week - Suggested activities and resources - Formative checkpoints - How each week builds on the previous

Personality: Organized, thorough, focused on student mastery rather than content coverage.

Experiment: Include a "flex day" recommendation for addressing gaps identified through formative assessment. [Constraints] - Ensure vertical alignment with previous and next grade level expectations - Include cross-curricular connection opportunities where natural - Balance direct instruction with student-centered activities

Content & Resource Creation

Generate quality educational materials customized for your students.

Reading Passage Leveler CRISP
Context: I'm a [Grade] teacher and I have a reading passage about [Topic] that is written at approximately [Current Level]. I need versions at multiple reading levels for my diverse classroom.

Request: Rewrite the following passage at three different Lexile levels: 1. Below grade level (approximately [X] Lexile) 2. On grade level (approximately [Y] Lexile) 3. Above grade level (approximately [Z] Lexile)

Instructions: - Maintain the same core information and learning objectives across all versions - Adjust vocabulary complexity, sentence structure, and text features appropriately - Keep the same paragraph structure so I can use them simultaneously in class - Bold key vocabulary words in each version

Style: Educational, engaging, age-appropriate.

Parameters: Each version should be approximately [X] words. Include 3 comprehension questions per version (also leveled). [Original Passage] [Paste passage here]
Interactive Activity Designer COSTAR
Context: You are designing hands-on learning activities for a [Grade Level] classroom studying [Topic].

Objective: Create an engaging, interactive activity that helps students understand [Specific Concept] through active participation rather than passive learning.

Style: Activity should feel like exploration/discovery, not worksheets.

Tone: Fun, engaging, with clear educational purpose.

Audience: [Age] year old students with varying attention spans and learning preferences.

Response: Design an activity that includes: 1. Catchy activity name 2. Learning objective (student-friendly language) 3. Materials needed (prioritize common classroom supplies) 4. Step-by-step instructions 5. Discussion questions during activity 6. Reflection/wrap-up component 7. Modifications for students who need support 8. Extension for students who finish early [Constraints] - Activity should take [X] minutes - Maximum [Y] students working together in groups - Must be feasible with limited budget/resources - Should generate a product students can reference later
Assessment Question Bank Creator CRISPE
Capacity: You are an assessment design expert familiar with Bloom's Taxonomy and Webb's Depth of Knowledge.

Role: Test development specialist for [Subject] education.

Insight: I need a question bank for assessing student understanding of [Topic/Unit]. I want questions at multiple cognitive levels to truly measure understanding, not just recall.

Statement: Create a bank of [X] questions distributed across cognitive levels: - 20% Recall/Remember (DOK 1) - 30% Understanding/Application (DOK 2) - 30% Analysis/Strategic Thinking (DOK 3) - 20% Synthesis/Extended Thinking (DOK 4) For each question, include: - The question itself - Cognitive level label - Answer key or rubric - Common misconception to watch for

Personality: Rigorous but fair, focused on measuring true understanding.

Experiment: Include 2-3 performance-based assessment options as alternatives to traditional questions. [Constraints] - Questions should be answerable by students who understood the material - Avoid trick questions or unnecessarily complex wording - Include a mix of formats: multiple choice, short answer, extended response

Student Support & Intervention

Identify struggling students early and provide targeted support.

Intervention Strategy Recommender COSTAR
Context: You are an intervention specialist helping a teacher support a struggling student.

Objective: Recommend targeted intervention strategies based on the following student profile: - Grade: [X] - Subject area of concern: [Subject] - Specific skill gap: [Describe what the student struggles with] - Current performance level: [Describe] - Learning style observations: [Visual/auditory/kinesthetic preferences] - Previous interventions tried: [List what hasn't worked]

Style: Evidence-based, practical, implementable by a classroom teacher without specialist training.

Tone: Supportive, solution-focused, strengths-based.

Audience: Classroom teacher with limited one-on-one time available.

Response: Provide: 1. Analysis of likely root causes for the struggle 2. Three specific intervention strategies (ranked by likely effectiveness) 3. How to implement each during regular class time 4. Progress monitoring approach (how to know if it's working) 5. Timeline expectations 6. When to escalate to specialist support [Constraints] - No student identifying information in prompt (use general descriptions) - Strategies should be feasible within normal classroom constraints - Include both immediate supports and longer-term skill building - Consider social-emotional factors that may be contributing
IEP Goal Progress Tracker CRISP
Context: I'm tracking progress on IEP goals for students in my classroom. I need help organizing observation data into meaningful progress reports.

Request: Help me structure a progress summary based on the following goal and observation data.

Instructions: Goal: [Paste the specific IEP goal] Baseline: [Starting performance level] Target: [End of year target] Recent observations: [List 3-5 recent data points with dates]

Style: Professional, objective, compliant with documentation standards.

Parameters: Generate a progress summary that includes: 1. Current performance level (with data support) 2. Trend analysis (improving, maintaining, declining) 3. Rate of progress toward goal 4. Recommended adjustments (if needed) 5. Next steps [Constraints] - Use objective, measurable language - Do not include any student identifying information - Frame in strengths-based language while being accurate about challenges - Include specific data points, not generalizations

Feedback & Assessment

Provide meaningful feedback faster—without sacrificing quality.

Constructive Feedback Generator CRISPE
Capacity: You are an expert at providing growth-oriented feedback that helps students improve while maintaining motivation.

Role: Writing coach and feedback specialist for [Grade Level] students.

Insight: I have a student [writing sample/assignment] that I need to provide feedback on. I want feedback that is specific, actionable, and encouraging—not just corrections.

Statement: Review the following student work and provide: 1. Two specific strengths (with examples from their work) 2. Two areas for growth (framed as "next steps" not criticisms) 3. One specific, actionable suggestion for their next draft/assignment 4. An encouraging closing comment

Personality: Warm but honest, focused on growth mindset, celebrates effort and progress.

Experiment: Include a "teacher note" suggesting a mini-lesson topic this student might benefit from. [Student Work] Assignment: [Describe the assignment] Grade Level Expectations: [What should a proficient response include?] [Paste student work here] [Constraints] - Feedback should be written directly to the student (use "you" language) - Keep feedback to 100-150 words maximum (students don't read long comments) - Focus on the most impactful improvements, not every error - Match language complexity to student's grade level
Rubric Builder COSTAR
Context: You are creating a standards-aligned rubric for a [Grade Level] [Subject] assignment.

Objective: Design a clear, student-friendly rubric for assessing [Assignment Description].

Style: Clear, specific language that students can use for self-assessment.

Tone: Professional but accessible to students and parents.

Audience: Students (for self-assessment), parents (for understanding expectations), teachers (for consistent grading).

Response: Create a rubric with: 1. 4-5 key criteria aligned to learning objectives 2. 4 performance levels (Exceeds/Meets/Approaching/Beginning) 3. Specific, observable descriptors for each cell 4. Point values that weight criteria appropriately 5. Student-friendly "I can" statements for self-check [Assignment Details] Assignment: [Describe assignment] Standards addressed: [List relevant standards] What matters most: [List 2-3 priority elements] [Constraints] - Language should be grade-appropriate - Descriptors should be specific enough that two teachers would score the same work similarly - Include at least one criterion related to process/effort, not just product - Avoid vague terms like "good" or "needs improvement"

Parent & Family Communication

Communicate effectively with families while saving time.

Parent Conference Prep CRISP
Context: I have parent-teacher conferences coming up and need to prepare talking points that are honest, constructive, and partnership-focused.

Request: Help me prepare a conference outline for discussing a student who [describe situation: excelling/struggling/behavioral concerns/mixed performance].

Instructions: Student profile (no names): [Grade, general academic standing, social dynamics, relevant context] Strengths to highlight: [List 2-3] Concerns to address: [List 2-3] Data points available: [Grades, assessments, observations]

Style: Professional, warm, collaborative.

Parameters: Create a conference outline including: 1. Opening (positive connection, set collaborative tone) 2. Celebration points (specific strengths with examples) 3. Growth areas (framed as opportunities, not failures) 4. Specific strategies for home support 5. Questions to ask the parent 6. Next steps and follow-up plan 7. Closing (reaffirm partnership) [Constraints] - Keep conference to [X] minutes - Prepare for potential defensive reactions with de-escalation language - Have specific examples ready for any claims made - Balance honesty with maintaining relationship
Newsletter/Update Generator CRISPE
Capacity: You are a skilled communicator who helps teachers maintain strong home-school connections.

Role: Family engagement specialist for [Grade Level] education.

Insight: I need to create a [weekly/monthly] classroom newsletter that keeps parents informed and engaged without overwhelming them.

Statement: Create a newsletter template/draft that includes: - Warm greeting - This week's learning highlights (brief, engaging) - Upcoming events/dates to remember - How to support learning at home (1-2 simple activities) - Celebration or positive classroom moment - Quick reminder or request

Personality: Warm, professional, enthusiastic but not overwhelming.

Experiment: Include a "question of the week" to spark dinner table conversations about learning. [This Week's Content] Topics covered: [List subjects/units] Upcoming: [Events, deadlines, etc.] Special mention: [Any classroom wins to share] Home support focus: [What skill could families reinforce?] [Constraints] - Keep to one page or less - Use bullet points for scannability - Include section headers for easy navigation - Translate-friendly (simple sentence structures) - No jargon or educational acronyms without explanation

Teaching AI Literacy

Help your students become responsible, critical users of AI tools.

AI Ethics Lesson Framework COSTAR
Context: You are helping a teacher develop an age-appropriate lesson on AI ethics and responsible use for [Grade Level] students.

Objective: Create a lesson that teaches students to think critically about when and how to use AI tools appropriately.

Style: Interactive, discussion-based, with real scenarios students can relate to.

Tone: Balanced (not fear-based or hype-based), exploratory, critical thinking focused.

Audience: [Age] year old students with [varying/some/limited] exposure to AI tools.

Response: Design a lesson including: 1. Hook: Real-world AI example students can relate to 2. Key concepts to cover: - What AI can and cannot do - When AI use is appropriate vs. inappropriate - How to verify AI-generated information - Ethical considerations (bias, attribution, authenticity) 3. Scenario-based discussions (3-4 "Would you use AI here?" situations) 4. Critical thinking activity: Evaluating AI output for accuracy 5. Class agreement/guidelines development activity 6. Reflection prompt [Constraints] - Age-appropriate examples and language - Balance caution with empowerment - Focus on ethical reasoning, not just rules - Include diverse perspectives on AI use
AI-Assisted Assignment Designer CRISPE
Capacity: You are an innovative educator who designs assignments where AI is a learning tool, not a shortcut.

Role: Curriculum designer specializing in AI-integrated learning experiences.

Insight: I want to create an assignment on [Topic] where students USE AI as part of the learning process, with the learning happening in how they interact with and evaluate the AI, not despite it.

Statement: Design an assignment that: 1. Explicitly allows/requires AI use at specific stages 2. Makes the learning visible through the process, not just the product 3. Requires critical evaluation of AI output 4. Has students add original thinking that AI cannot provide 5. Includes reflection on the AI collaboration experience

Personality: Forward-thinking, focused on skill development, comfortable with appropriate AI use.

Experiment: Include a "AI disclosure" requirement where students document how they used AI and what they learned from the interaction. [Assignment Context] Subject: [Subject] Topic: [Specific topic] Learning objectives: [What should students be able to do after?] Skills to develop: [Critical thinking, writing, analysis, etc.] [Constraints] - The assignment should be impossible to complete with ONLY AI - Human judgment and original thinking must be essential components - Include clear guidelines on acceptable AI use for this assignment - Build in accountability for AI-generated portions

Quick Prompt Starters

Copy and customize these quick prompts for common teaching tasks.

Explain Like I'm [Age]

"Explain [complex concept] in terms a [age/grade] year old would understand. Use analogies from their everyday life like [context: video games, sports, social situations]."

Generate Practice Problems

"Create [X] practice problems for [skill] at [difficulty level]. Include an answer key and explain common mistakes students make on each."

Discussion Question Generator

"Generate [X] discussion questions about [text/topic] at different levels of Bloom's Taxonomy. Include 2 recall, 2 analysis, and 2 evaluation questions."

Vocabulary in Context

"Create a short story or passage (approximately [X] words) that uses these vocabulary words in context: [list words]. The story should be engaging for [grade level] students and relate to [topic/theme]."

Misconception Identifier

"What are the most common misconceptions students have about [topic]? For each misconception, explain why students think this way and suggest a teaching strategy to address it."

Real-World Connection Finder

"Help me connect [curriculum topic] to real-world situations that [grade level] students care about. Give me 5 specific examples I can use to make this topic relevant."

Best Practices for Educators Using AI

The AI-Empowered Educator's Principles
  • AI assists, you decide. You know your students. AI doesn't.
  • Review everything. AI makes mistakes. Always verify before using with students.
  • Customize for your context. Generic AI output needs your expertise to become great teaching.
  • Model transparency. Let students know when you've used AI to create materials.
  • Protect privacy. Never put student names or identifying information into AI tools.
  • Save your time for relationships. Let AI handle the mechanical so you can focus on the human.

You're Not Being Replaced

AI cannot inspire, connect, or truly understand your students. It can help you grade faster, plan smarter, and differentiate more effectively—so you have more energy for the irreplaceable human work of teaching.
